The Island of Glass

loregeographyarchitectureglass-island

Glass is an island roughly half the size of Honshu, triangular in shape, with a long mountain range on its counterclockwise side and a smaller cluster of peaks to its clockwise point. The land between forms a raised valley — temperate, fertile, and increasingly scarred by war.

The Structure of Glass Society

The official religious & national stance is that Glassians are free, and furthermore that Glassians are equal in death — through reincarnation. This status quo was established & enforced by Kain, an immortal tyrant who could not be challenged by anyone who sought to overthrow him. This force of will sustained a free perspective on the world that couldn’t be developed elsewhere.

Everywhere else on Aia is caught in the Bronze Age optimization cycle. Every other god & king on Glass is subject to mortal power concerns — evolutionary pressures to optimize the cycles of fertility, war, & slavery. This extended not just to temporal rulers but also to gods, who (being little more than super-powered & sometimes ageless mortals themselves) were no better than their contemporary rulers in requiring maximum possible extraction of labor from increasingly dehumanized serfs & peasants to compete in war.

Worse, the impact of conquest on personal power creates a cycle of Flower War-style low-intensity conflict. Because men only grow powerful when exposed to war, most societies advance towards ensuring a constant state of conflict with neighbours in order to avoid producing weak heirs whose rule can be challenged by internal or external enemies. The most accomplished conquerors are capable of fighting a thousand swordsmen toe-to-toe & winning single-handedly. Their heir has no such power unless he replicates his feats.

The Commons loved Kain. Kain was not a moral or lawful man — he was a flippant, arbitrary murderer — but he was a stabilizing force with no dependencies & little to ask from his subjects other than that conflict be kept low. The common man, with nothing to gain from ruling his fellow Glassian, finds freedom in the yoke placed upon his masters.

But the magistrates hated him. For every ten thousand commoners, an elite of exceptional administrative & military talent was being exposed to the arbitrary & absurdist rulings of a man focused on keeping them chained with as little personal effort expended as possible. Every single lord plotted silently against Kain — silence for fear, but of intent nonetheless.

Social Classes

The current distribution of Glass’s population is a snapshot of a society coming apart. Kain kept everyone roughly in their place; without him, the machinery of class is grinding loudly. Each entry below gives the percentage, the human reality behind it, and the conflict it creates at the table.

The Commons (85%)

  • How to enter: If you’re a Glassian & not a Governor, you’re here by default.
  • How to escape: Become a Slave via debt, find a Magician willing to train you, or catch the eye of a Governor.

If you’re a Glassian & not a Governor, this is you.

Commoners (70%). Labourers, farmers, soldiers, entertainers, the destitute — anyone who sells raw labour for a living. Many aspire to become Burghers. Politically, they are the reason Kain’s system worked: they had nothing to gain from ruling anyone, and Kain demanded little from them. Now, in the civil war, they are the ones being conscripted, taxed, and displaced.

At the table: Commoners are the backdrop of every negotiation, every recruitment drive, every battle’s aftermath. A Commoner’s Mood shifts based on who fed them last and who burned their field. They are pragmatic, not ideological.

Burghers (15%). Entrepreneurs, artisans, merchants, moneylenders — the economic engine. Burghers sit in an uncomfortable middle: rich enough to matter, not noble enough to be safe. They are the primary source of new Magistrates (their children are sent to be educated into the bureaucracy).

At the table: Burghers control the supply lines. If the Party needs equipment, information, or a place to lie low, they deal with Burghers. A Burgher’s loyalty is to their ledger first and their faction second.

Outlaws & Foreigners

How to enter: Lose your land and take up a spear. Flee the law or be exiled. Be shipwrecked on a shore that has no path to citizenship.

How to escape: Buy a patron’s favor. Marry into a Burgher line. Make yourself too useful to prosecute. Find a boat that can leave Glass, if such a thing still exists.

Roughly one in ten Glassians live outside the law’s protection (8% outlaws, 3% foreigners). The reason why determines what they have & what they can lose.

Mercenaries are tolerated only as long as the war pays. Between contracts, a mercenary is just a vagrant with a weapon. A lord who hired you yesterday will hang you tomorrow if you are caught stealing from his granary. The only thing that keeps a mercenary safe is the war not ending.

Criminals are safe only as long as they stay invisible. Bandits, smugglers, and murderers live by one rule: do not become someone else’s bounty. A criminal with a patron is protected; one without is a reward. The war has been very good for criminal business, but the same chaos that creates customers also attracts wolves.

Foreigners are safe only as long as they are useful. Non-Glassians have no legal standing, no path to citizenship, and no recourse if they are robbed, imprisoned, or killed. A foreigner with a lord’s favor can thrive. One without it does not survive long. Every party will eventually have to decide whether that bothers them.

See also: Gangster class, Army factions, The Commons’ Revolt

Servants & Slaves (10%)

Servants (5%). Hereditary household staff, descended from prisoners of war taken in past invasions. Coralers, salamanders, harpies, foreign humans — never Glassian by birth. Strict laws protect their rights (they cannot be killed or maimed without cause), but they are not considered Glassian and can never become so.

  • Enter: Be captured in war and assigned to a household, or be born to a Servant.
  • Exit: Persuade your master to free you, or flee into the outlaw population.

Slaves (5%). Indentured labourers working without wages for a fixed term. The indentured population has nearly doubled since the war began. Unlike Servants, Glassians can become Slaves — voluntarily (to pay a debt) or involuntarily (by court order). An indenture is a debt, and debts can be sold.

  • Enter: Owe debts you cannot pay. A court assigns you to servitude; a creditor buys your contract.
  • Exit: Serve your term, buy out your contract, or flee (the war has made this much easier).

At the table: Slavery in Glass is transactional, not chattel-based (except for Servants, who are a separate hereditary caste). A Slave met on the road might be an escaped debtor, a fugitive, or someone whose contract was bought by a faction your party is at war with. Servants are a mirror of their household’s power: a well-kept Servant means a secure Lord; a mistreated one means trouble coming.

The Magicians (~1%)

Jesters (~1%). The shrine-keeping priests who negotiate between Glass and its gods. Mistrusted by everyone, tolerated because nobody else can do the job. Stereotypically a girl’s role, though the profession is evenly split. Jesters are the only thing preventing the gods from simply wiping out the population — and everyone knows it.

  • Enter: Be born into a Jester family, or persuade a Jester to train you.
  • Exit: Abandon your duties. But nobody can truly flee the gods.

Magicians (rare). Warlocks, cultists, foreign-trained spellcasters — anyone who uses magic outside the Jester system. Technically illegal under Imperial Law, but this ban is unevenly enforced. Magicians include the Solar Cult and most foreign schools.

  • Enter: Find a Magician willing to teach you. Open to any class.
  • Exit: The law catches up eventually, or you become powerful enough to ignore it.

At the table: A Jester is a neutral party in any conflict — harming one is bad luck and worse politics. A Magician is a wildcard: useful, dangerous, and always one step ahead of the law. Both can be hired, but the price is never coin.

The State (~3%)

Magistrates (2%). The scholar-bureaucracy. They run the imperial organs — courts, treasuries, archives, licensing. Alchemists and book-printers must be companies managed by a Magistrate. In theory, their position is at-will and merit-based. In practice, powerful Magistrates command networks of allies among Lords and Burghers, and their offices often become hereditary in all but name.

  • Enter: Be elected or appointed. Burgher families send their children into Magistrate education.
  • Exit: Get fired, lose a political struggle, or be caught in a corruption scandal.

Lords (1%). The scholar-warrior aristocracy. Every Lord traces their bloodline to Kain through one of his many bastard children — after two millennia of rule, this claim is more legal fiction than genealogy, but it is the foundation of all land rights on Glass. Lords command armies, hold cities as capitals, and fight small wars over titles and territory. The position is hereditary and male-only by common law (exceptions exist, but they are the kind of exception that starts a war).

  • Enter: Be born into a House, or persuade a Lord to adopt you (a flimsy bloodline justification is expected).
  • Exit: Lose a war. Have your titles seized. Abdicate.

Imperial Family (negligible). The current ruling house, descended from the Saint Emperor. Absolutely patrilineally hereditary.

  • Enter: Be born into the Imperial House.
  • Exit: You cannot.

At the table: Magistrates are the gatekeepers of legitimacy — documents, permissions, legal cover. A Lord is a walking faction: their name is an army, their word is a treaty, and their death is a succession crisis. Dealing with either means dealing with the apparatus of the state, which is slow, corrupt, and dangerous.

Political Landscape

Distributed feudalism (three Houses + vassals) with somewhat antiquated but regenerating military traditions. A fortification arms race is underway, driven by siege dominance and harpy intelligence networks. Intelligence competition (harpy subsidies) dominates the non-kinetic battlefield.

Military Landscape

A limited hero-worship culture — Kain suppressed the typical Aian tendency for nobility to become wuxia-esque gods. Siege-focused doctrine with large but by now somewhat antiquated fortresses is commonplace. Ritualized combat seasons structure the annual cycle of violence.

Economic Landscape

Agricultural base with magical intensification. Slave labour for fortifications and armies. Tributary extraction (when the empire existed). Boom-bust cycles driven by unsustainable harpy subsidies.

Religious Landscape

War gods are gaining prominence due to the civil war. Rising temple prostitution for mass human production (orchestrated by the Jesters?) threatens to destabilise the old reincarnationist orthodoxy.

Magician Demography

Glass has approximately 1 magician per 100 people. At most, 5 of every 100 can practically be magicians — in the sense of possessing the temperament, intelligence, & skill required to do so.
