Translator's notes

HD & HP HD is physical & spiritual durability. Humans are 2 HD & harpies, salamanders, & Coralers are all 1 HD. Otherwise, we stick kinda close to OSR, but maybe add 1 HD to normal assumptions. All HD are d6. HD ½ = 2 HP & halved rolls. HD 0 = always 0 HP. this isn’t lethal but it’s instant death if you take a hit. HP is always (HD × 4).

Level We assume Level is 0 (& don’t bother writing it down) unless this entry is SPECIFICALLY for a ruler of a Faction (i.e. it’s a “Duke” or “General” or “Business Magnate” entry) in which case defer to core rules’ Level table to determine average assumed size.

Armor (AP) Armor indicates absorption via Armor Points. Strictly speaking, Armor Points is a multiple of Power (HD + Level) added as an additional HP-equivalent & is based on the quality of gear. Leather is Power × 1, Chain is × 2, Plate is × 4. We can conceivably have bigger Armor bonuses for better Armor materials … but probably not.

That said, we don’t show the Armor multiplier value since it can be worked out from just the final AP number. To get the original Armor number, you can divide AP by Power.

Entities that have high HP but low HD (i.e. they are of weak character/strength of mind) should have an Armor bonus instead of getting extra HD. In addition, beasts that are literally heavily armored (like dragons) should have AP. We’d call this natural armor but we have no reason to make a distinction. In mechanical terms, some attacks can pierce Armor & attack HP directly & Armor provided from gear doesn’t heal from rest. Those are the main practical differences.

Calculating BR As per the rules in Battles, under Battle Rating (p. @).

Size Size is the cylindrical area you can fit into while fighting. Any value over 5’ should be rounded to 5’ increments. Standard sizes are 0’, 1’, 2’, 5’, 10’, 15’, 20’, 30’, 40’, & 60’. Arbitrarily larger sizes are also acceptable.

Weight & Max. Load Weight is measured in stone (#). 1# = 10lb. We accept ½#, ¼#, & N/A. Should generally be rounded to increments of 3# where possible. Weight should reflect realistic mass (i.e. “this thing is 1,000 pounds” = it should be Weight 100#). Max. Load is carry capacity & should generally be 2/3 of Weight unless your knowledge of the creature’s biology for some reason makes an exception worthwhile.

Speed scores Run, Fly, Swim & Dig (in that order) should be in 5’ increments wherever possible. Don’t list Speeds that a creature doesn’t have. Humans don’t have a Dig score. Possessing a score indicates a natural capacity for it; Humans can swim, but they don’t have Swim 15’. Climbing (i.e. like a spider) is not a separate score; it is a Feature that lets you use Run to climb. Humans move at Run 25’. We treat 5’ as 1” on a combat surface normally.

Weapon & attack design philosophy Classic B/X or OSR design gives various monsters a bunch of redundant/similar attacks. bite+bite+claw? nigga pls, they all do Slash damage. A weapon is meaningfully differentiated if its damage, type, usage patterns, or special effects (i.e. range, armor pierce, burning) matter. if there’s no reason to ever choose between two different weapons (natural or otherwise), why separate them?

All weapons are phrased as Features tagged with (Weapon). Natural weapons are tagged (Natural Weapon). Weapons are implicitly melee unless a range is provided (in feet, or as “LoS” for line-of-sight effects).

Weapons usually don’t grant multiple Strikes except as particularly special abilities. Extra Strikes are a separate Feature & most natural weapons should be combined into one big weapon if they have no differentiation.

T/N: Feature design philosophy

  • Simplicity over fidelity. Avoid fiddly conditionals or mental spoon load. Strive for obvious/mnemonic conditions if you need conditionals.
  • Avoid referencing specific weaponry unless it’s essential to the Feature. (“Natural weapons” is better than “claws”; think like, if a PC gets this power, would it make no sense? sometimes i.e. mind flayer tentacles, it’s unavoidable and that’s okay. other times, you can correct for it.)
  • If it only works in rare circumstances or just grants a +whatever bonus to niche actions, consider cutting it or merging it with another Feature. Each Feature should be ideally punchy and central to the thing’s identity.

T/N: Optional: GM Prep Tables

Determine the most difficult part of using the creature. Basically, ask yourself “what’s the hardest thing for a GM to make up on the spot that will almost always be needed with this creature?” pick the top 0-3. Sphinxes need riddles & GMs never have a book of riddles on-hand. We want to maximize utility. a prep table should NEVER consist of:

  • Mundanities (the dwarf has a family)
  • easy stereotypes (the dwarf is a miner with a beard & is named greyhammer & lives in a hill)
  • random bullshit (the dwarf is a gay cake-maker)

it should be structured, difficult-to-ad-hoc content with extremely high conceptual density. whatever’s critical to its identity & hard to come up with. “Do the hard work for the GM.”

some ideas: “What is this thing doing?” “What is its nature?” “Cause/casus belli for [stupid shit they do but that’s difficult to wrap your head around as a human who doesn’t think like they do]”. victims/slaves/captives, symbiotes/parasites & other common/universal relationships, “why the fuck would we ever bother this thing/who’s prompting us into it & why”, “common/interesting variants or very similar creatures you can make by changing this thing’s name & giving it some minor tweaks that you can contain in 160-200 characters”, “organizational structure”, “premade encounters”, “hazards that are required to go along with this entity to make it work conceptually”, “weird weapons & armor”, “WHY the FUCK does this thing EXIST, who did this and why???”, “set of specific highly interesting & thematically appropriate loot”, “legendary/mythical powers associated with this creature or with special/unique versions of it”.

write tables as proper Markdown tables. don’t bother giving them headings since you can just put the heading into the table itself like the examples below. you can include multiple columns if they all go together i.e. “Gem color” “Value” “Power” or whatever. we only use d6 dice, so 1d6 for most tables, or 2d6 if you need lots of results or weighted results. you can also use d66 numbered double per entry i.e. 11–12, 13–14 […] 65–66 to get an 18-entry table w/ a flat curve … but you’d better have a really exciting reason to make an 18-entry table.

T/N: Regarding When Eaten entries

Players can eat monsters to gain better stats (if it had better stats than them) & their Features or Facts (as mutations, kinda). If a creature has other unusual effects or characteristics related to its consumption (i.e. some beasts are HORRIBLY CURSED or KNOWN TO CURE DISEASE or THE FEAST OF KINGS), that’s what this section is for. It should NOT be included for anything of blisteringly mundane quality. Basically, if you’re describing a normal meal, this section should not be here. A goat entry doesn’t deserve a When Eaten section (unless it’s a Scapegoat & eating it transfers all the awful sins the goat bears onto the consumer or some shit).

write tables as proper Markdown tables. don’t nest rolls inside them. Don’t use Saves, just do straight 1d6 or 2d6 tables with entries weighted appropriately.

T/N: Other general design principles

  • Try not to use funny names where English words or calques would be better. Names should be mnemonic where possible; trivial to remember.
  • Natural weapons are not Features for the sake of BR. No weapon is.
  • We do some lumping. Basically, ask yourself if a creature could be achieved with a very minor edit to the stats of this creature. A name edit, a tweak to a stat or two, maybe one extra ability. The giant badger is a slight edit of the wolf. The dolphin is a slight edit of the seal.
    • Tables of variant creatures should never include the base creature. If a GM wanted to use the creature as-is, he’d just do that.
  • Generally speaking, pack hunters don’t need abilities that specifically represent their skill for hunting in packs; this is implicit to the way the Battles system works. A pack has a huge advantage in claiming tactical bonuses (terrain, perception, flanking, maneuverability, morale) which can potentially add up to a +4 collectively (on a 2d6 Save!). This could allow a relatively small pack to fight on even ground against a creature with up to 4× more BR than the pack does. Ergo, don’t give a “pack hunter” bonus unless it’s really, sincerely justified.
  • AP must always be granted by an Armor score. If you want to give a creature an ablative pool of HP somehow (i.e. a robot having parts with separate HP), you should give it fixed HP and/or HD.
  • Immunities, Resistances, and Weaknesses are conveyed via Features because we want them to be copiable by alchemy. (Every Feature is potentially an ability that a player could obtain.)
  • Unusual/magical equipment is listed as a Feature, tagged with (Equipment), i.e. “Magic Shield (Equipment)“.
    • Unusual/magical weapons are similarly tagged as {Weapon}.
    • Unusual natural weapons are tagged as (Natural Weapon).
    • Class Features (i.e. derived from profession or knowledge) are labeled (Class) & are otherwise presumed to be Racial (or natural) Features.

Description, Wants, Intellect, & Morality can all be invoked as Facts where necessary.

Playing a Glassian Commoner

These are not medieval serfs. Kain’s long reign suppressed the worst excesses of intermediate lordship. Glassian commoners are commercially literate, own property, haggle confidently, & hold strong opinions about the quality of their local products versus the next village’s. They remember prosperity & are furious about losing it.

Spirits are landlords. Commoners negotiate with river spirits the way you negotiate with your landlords. They set out offering plates at tree roots, consult the shrine-keeper before building an extension, & teach their children which mushrooms have spirits in them.

Glassians know their trade, their village, their local spirits & rumors from the last trader or harpy courier who came through. They don’t know politics or how magic works, but they’re not stupid. Everything they know works because it worked for the last thousand years.

Glassians are not neutral. Under competing warlords, commoners hedge between factions the way a trader diversifies debts. They hide grain, maintain polite relations with whoever’s currently armed, & gossip as intelligence-gathering. They will sell information for safety. Their children are simultaneously their retirement, labor force, & army.

Lunarians

The Lunarians do not have an “economy” & are not best understood as a Faction, but rather instead as a den of powerful spirits (the Royal Family) overseeing a collection of pets (the moon rabbits) in a kind of post-scarcity bubble society (the Lunar Kingdom) on a bizarre & hostile world (the Moon) full of their scattered, experimental, discarded, or disabled weaponry & utilities (the Lunarian flora & fauna, plus their artifacts).

The moon rabbits rarely deal with the Lunar ecosystem directly. They have spirits who keep the worst of the Moon’s hazards away from their villages.
