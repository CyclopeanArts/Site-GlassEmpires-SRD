@ This playmode is applicable to when you’re adjudicating the adventure & movement of a handful of characters through dangerous environments.

tldr: everything in this section is specifically for “how does [my character] interact with [the world] in a way that makes sense?”. it’s for resolving basic low-level tensions. it’s VERY STANDARD and covered in almost every RPG, and it is therefore in need of being SEPARATED by a GREAT WALL from our more interesting stuff about Factions. @@@

@ Encumbrance

10lb is 1 stone (1#). Everything is weighed in stone.

Characters have a Max. Load indicating how many stone they can carry safely (assuming they have storage for those goods). Each hand can hold up to 1# independently from your Max. Load. The impact of encumbrance depends on how much of a character’s Max. Load is occupied:

  • Up to half (rounded up): No effect.
  • Up to full: Suffer the Encumbered Condition.
  • More than full: You cannot usefully move.

Characters can Save to deadlift up to twice their Max. Load for up to 1 Round (10 seconds), after which they must Save again.

Small Items: Most small items weighing less than 1# but more than 2lb weigh ¼#. Smaller items are weightless. Items sold as bundles in the marketplace weigh their listed weight for a bundle of that quantity.

Item Slots & Cards: On your sheet, Max. Load is represented by 1½” item slots on the edge of the sheet. 1 slot is 1#. Items weighing ¼# can be stacked up to 4 per slot. Print out item cards & attach them with paperclips, or just write in the spaces with a pencil.

Encumbrance Condition

While Encumbered, characters halve their effective Speed scores while measuring long-distance travel. Short-term movement rates such as in combat are not affected.

In addition, being Encumbered is a −1 penalty on Saves made for dexterity or agility. This does not include Strike Saves made for Attacks.

@ Move: Foresight

PCs are generally assumed to be competent & to also notice a variety of details & niche planning that their players may not have had the time or focus to realize. For this reason, when presented with challenges or obstacles that weren’t explicitly foreseen but that may have potentially come up in off-screen planning or logistical actions, players may assert foresight by stating an action their PC made in advance to deal with the situation & making a Save to determine if their PC was active, wise, or perceptive enough to have done so.

The criteria for foresight is as follows:

  • Doesn’t require knowing anything the PCs had absolutely no way of knowing.
  • Doesn’t require things or events that are absolutely impossible or that contradict anything that’s already been established within the game world.
  • No other PC has attempted to have foresight over this specific obstacle.

You may receive a penalty on your Save depending on how complex & unlikely the action you want to take was, which fills in for the ordinary situational penalties a GM may apply on a case-by-case basis:

  • No penalty: Ordinary action which you had ample opportunity to perform.
    • Was the action trivial to perform?
    • If it wasn’t trivial to perform, was it at least cheap & the benefits foreseeable?
  • −2 penalty: Complex action or unlikely opportunities.
    • Does the plan have several moving parts?
    • Does it rely on additional events which weren’t guaranteed?
    • Did the preparation rely on Retainers or other servants with an incomplete grasp of the motives at play, or a reason to interfere?
  • −4 penalty: Exceptionally elaborate, specialized, or implausible actions or research.
    • Does it rely on unlikely facts about the world that are unestablished or uncorroborated — facts that are made up on the spot by the player?
    • Did the players have at best a very tenuous opportunity to acquire information critical to the foresight?
    • Secretly, by GM’s judgement: Is the Player relying on an entity that wanted to betray or sabotage them & that would have seen this sabotage as worth the risk?

While most normal situational penalties are wrapped up in this scale, characters may still add various bonuses from their Facts and other relevant modifiers to the Save.

ResultForesight Save Effect
Crit & Pass
8–11		You planned in advance what you needed to overcome this specific obstacle more or less as stated.
Seven
7		You have something similar to but insufficient for the task at hand, originally intended for a different contingency — at least enough for a +1 bonus to a Save, if that’s what you’re trying to overcome. If no partial preparation is obviously possible, you suffer a Failure instead.

Conditions

Conditions are temporary Facts that affect characters or objects in various ways. Most are harmful, but some can be helpful in certain situations. Conditions last until narratively countered (i.e. being Prone is countered by taking the Stand Up Action) or until a specified duration elapses.

Generally, Conditions behave like Facts with special rules attached. They can be invoked by players & GMs for ±1 modifiers on applicable Saves.

Characters cannot suffer more than one Condition of the same type at once. If they would & the new duration is longer than the current one, the duration of their existing Condition is extended to meet it.

Damage, Incapacitation & Death

Characters lose 1 HP per point of Damage dealt to them. If a character is reduced to less than 0 HP, the character is Incapacitated. (Characters can have infinite negative HP.)

Incapacitated characters are unconscious & essentially useless.

When a character becomes Incapacitated or has just lost additional HP while already Incapacitated, if their current negative HP exceeds their Power, they die. Otherwise, they receive a Wound (p. @).

If an Incapacitated character is left alone for an hour, he may Save to revive himself. If he fails, he dies. If a character arrives to assist him before that hour is up, he may add a +2 bonus to his Save for being assisted & may also borrow the assistant’s Facts (i.e. Lessons pertaining to being a doctor, etc.) while making his Save.

Funerals

The survivors of characters who die may have a Jester (p. @) host a funeral (p. @) to alert a psychopomp to the dead soul in need of ferrying.

Armor & Armor Points

Armor is a score (usually from 1 to 4) granted by equipment or Features that lets a character gain Armor Points (hereafter AP), which is essentially a backup pool of HP.

If a character has AP & takes Damage, they may deplete their AP before subtracting Damage from their HP. If you run out of AP while absorbing a blow, the remaining Damage spills over to & is subtracted from your HP.

The amount of AP granted by Armor is variable, because Armor is a multiplier of Power (which is HD + Level). If you have 4 Armor and 8 Power (i.e. if you’re a HD 2 human at Lv6), your Armor will grant you 4 × 8 = 32 AP. In practical terms, with 4 HP being typical per HD for most cases, a full set of plate armor will double your effective HP.

Characters can have multiple Armor scores from many sources, but only the best is ever used for determining how much AP is gained. AP is reset to its maximum value whenever a character Rests (p. @).

Healing AP

Whenever you rest, you also heal AP. See the Resting Move (p. @) for details.

Removing Armor

If you remove equipment that’s giving you an Armor score, you must reduce your AP to your next highest Armor score (if any) times your Power. After you put on equipment with an Armor score, you must heal HP from resting in order to reset your AP.

Damage Types

Damage may have 1 or more Types. The 7 Types are:

  • Acid: Corrosive burns.
  • Electric: Powerful electrical currents.
  • Ice: Hypothermic exposure.
  • Fire: Hyperthermic exposure.
  • Mind: Psychic, mental & emotional trauma.
  • Physical: Slashing, puncturing, & blunt-force trauma damage.
  • Poison: Internal toxins.

If no Damage Type is specified, it is said to be Typeless Damage.

Resistances, Weaknesses, & Immunities

Characters may be Weak, Resistant, or Immune to certain Damage Types.

  • Resistance halves all Damage of that Type, rounded down.
  • Weakness doubles all Damage of that Type.
  • Immunity neutralizes all Damage of a given Type.

If a source of Damage has more than 1 Type (designated with a slash, i.e. “6 Ice/Fire Damage”), the weakest resistance status is always used.

For example, if a character took 6 Ice/Fire Damage & had Ice Resistance while being Weak to Fire, the character would take 12 Fire Damage (instead of taking 3 Ice Damage).

Typeless Damage

Typeless Damage cannot be resisted.

Move: Resting

Resting is an extended Move performable by both individual characters & whole Factions. It is the primary way of healing.

When you choose to Rest, your character (or Faction) remains idle (performing no strenuous physical or mental activity) until the end of the intended period or until interrupted by an intruding force. Generally, this will involve a timeskip.

Duration

The Deadline is set by the group Leader as the intended date of resuming activity.

On the Date

If the resting period was at least an hour, then at the end, for each involved character, count how many of the following conditions were true for the period:

  • No Wounds.
  • Good food & drink available (travel rations don’t count, but monsters cooked fresh do).
  • Rested for 8 hours or more (i.e. overnight) in a bed (sleeping bags count).
    • If this condition is met, you may also restore all exhausted Spell Dice (p. @).
  • Rested for a day or more.
  • Rested for a week or more.
  • Rested for a month or more.

Take the number of true conditions & multiply it by [Power]. If the result is greater than the character’s current HP, the character’s HP is increased to match the result.

Repairing Armor Points

When you heal any amount of HP by resting, you may reset your AP to be equal to your best Armor times your Power, up to at most as much HP was healed from the rest.

Movement

Rough Terrain

Stairs & hills steeper than 45° are Rough Terrain & halve your Run score. The GM may also rule certain other kinds of terrain (such as tightropes, which may require a Save to cross successfully, or narrow cracks) as being Rough Terrain due to difficulty of navigation.

Rough Terrain usually doesn’t matter unless you’re in a Skirmish (p. @) or are very pressed for time.

Climbing

Ladders & Ropes

Ladders, hand-holds & other implements made to be climbed can be climbed at a speed no greater than one quarter your Run score.

Free Climbing

You may Save to travel up a rough surface steeper than 60° to a point that you can see. For each additional 10° of slope (including if it becomes an overhang), add a −1 penalty. Add a bonus for the time spent studying the route:

  • ≥1 minute: +1
  • ≥10 minutes: +2
  • ≥1 hour: +3

Climbing is too slow for combat if you lack a Feature that grants climbing ability.

ResultClimbing Save Effect
Pass & Crit

Failure
3–6		Fall at the middle of your climb.

If your climb is longer than you can see (i.e. due to light source range), you must Save repeatedly.

Falling Damage

When a character falls, determine the distance fallen.

  • 500’ or less: You may not take any actions excepting those that are essentially instantaneous, & take ([Weight] + your carried equipment’s weight in #) Physical Damage upon striking the ground, capped to at most the distance in feet you fell (if you fall 3’, you can’t take more than 3 Physical Damage).
  • Greater than 500’: You take ([Weight] + your carried equipment’s weight in #) × 5 Physical Damage upon striking the ground & may take 1 Major Action during your fall for each complete 500 feet that you fall.

Water reduces fall damage by 5. Cushions (such as hay) reduce fall damage by 10.

If yo or an item lands on a character, they take the same amount of damage.

Catching an Idiot

Always succeeds, if you’re in a position to do so. Split Damage equally between everyone in a position to help.

Condition: Prone

Being on the ground is called being Prone, and this Condition covers crawling, sitting, & any other form of limited movement. A character’s effective Speed scores (& height) is quartered while Prone.

The Prone Condition counts as a Fact for any Save where it’s relevant (i.e. prone characters have a hard time defending against melee strikes, but an easier time against long-distance ranged attacks).

Jumping

In tense situations, you may make a Save to attempt a jump. Gain a +2 bonus if you spend at least half your Run score on a straight running start.

If your intended destination is less than your actual distance traveled, you fall at the furthest point you manage.

High Jumps

Measuring from the ground where you jump, you can reach up to twice your Size for a pull-up grab. You may also perform a vault over an object of up to your Size if you treat the vertical height of the object as distance traveled for movement’s sake.

Rounding Speed Scores

Always round Speed scores (whenever divided) to the nearest 5’ increment.

Tidbits

These are pieces of trivia that have occasionally proved useful for calculating rulings.

Excavation

Characters can move cubic feet of earth equal to their Max. Load in # per hour, assuming appropriate tools are possessed. The rate is halved for improvised tools, and quartered for a lack of them. Dressed stone brick takes twice as long to excavate.

Making Rations

Creatures produce ¼# in rations per 1# of weight when skinned, smoked, & salted. Salting consumes ¼# of salt per 1# of rations.

Move: Travel

Travel by a Party or mobile Factions (such as Armies) is resolved via free movement tracked on a map of the campaign world by the GM.

In order to Travel, you must have a route & destination in mind. The route may be a path or a direction. The destination can be a literal place or a time-based trigger such as “three days north”. Announce both to the GM.

Travel Procedure

  1. The GM determines if the route can actually take you to the destination.
  2. The GM measures out the path to the destination & determines the total distance that either must be traveled (to reach a target place). or that will be traveled (to travel for a certain period of time).
    • If the party is following an erroneous route, the destination is the distance that the Party would have to travel before obviously realizing something is wrong (i.e. encountering an obviously out-of-the-way landmark or passing enough time to have reached the destination if on the correct course).
  3. The GM determines if there’s possible overlap with any other Faction movement based on relative Speeds & routes taken. If there appears to be a point of encounter, the GM (secretly) alters the destination to instead be the encounter & recalculates travel distance & time.
  4. The GM sets a (secret) Deadline as the time at which the destination will be reached.
  5. For each week of travel (or at the midpoint, if less than one), the GM calls for PCs in a traveling party to check for an Exhaustion Save if necessary & simultaneously rolls on the Discoveries table.
  6. When the Deadline for a Travel Move to finish comes up, the GM rolls a Travel Save for the party.

Exhaustion Save

Each week, each PC in a party should check if any of the following conditions are true:

  • A PC or their followers are carrying less than 2# of supplies each & are also not in a farmland, pastures, or otherwise settled region.
    • None at all.
  • The terrain is barrens/wasteland where food & water cannot be found easily.
  • Your mounts are unfit for the biome (horses in the desert, camels in the arctic).

For each true condition, each affected PC in the Party must perform an Exhaustion Save. Add leadership, quartermastery & hunting skills as +1 bonuses.

What if not all NPCs in the group are Followers of a PC?

If some members of the group are not Followers of any PC, one PC must on their Save treat them as their Followers for the sake of determining their fates, so as to prevent them from being immune to exhaustion or starvation.

But I want food & water to matter on a general basis.

And it does. With 20lb of gear dedicated towards survival, people & animals have enough in excess capacity & storage to escape starvation & malnutrition 95% of the time. Instead of slowing down the game by explicitly modeling this, we just ask if you have that equipment & if you don’t (or if you’re in a desert where things are harsher), we punish you based on the rate of starvation you’d see versus your PC’s leadership & survival skills.

This may not be to your preference, but God forbid we make people do the rocket equation for equipping an army every time they wanted to go somewhere. I felt that this was beneath the scope of the game. It’s actually the same reason we don’t generally track discrete quantities of cash. When you’re rich & powerful, you rely more on the heuristics of cashflow versus expenditures, not on nickels & dimes; penny-pinching is for the poor.

Discoveries Table

The GM rolls once on the Discoveries table per week (or part thereof) that a Party spends traveling.

1d6Discoveries
1–2Encounter. An encounter with another traveler, travelers, or local Monsters. There is a 2-in-6 chance it is actually a Lair (mark it on the map as a Location), unless this Route already passes by a Lair.
3–4Location. A new formerly unmarked Location is discovered. If the determined Location closely matches the identity of a Location that’s already marked along the Route (i.e. if you roll a tower, & there’s a castle on the way), there’s no new Location; it’s the existing one, instead.
5Omen. Roll for 2 Encounters & determine the most likely thing that would happen if the two of them met. Traces of that outcome are discovered by the Party. A pointer indicating the identity of the first Encounter rolled remains (i.e. tattered banner, dead body, viscous slime, dropped letter, observant/curious local, paw-print) provided that anyone in the Party would recognize what it meant.
6Nothing.

In geographic terms, each Discovery is spaced out more or less evenly across the route.

The GM’s Guide has instructions on how to create & manage tables of Encounters & Locations, as well as generators to help produce both.

Measuring distance & time taken

Characters can travel ~1 mile per day across a medium for each 1’ of Speed score they possess. A human with a Run of 25’ can travel 25 miles per day. A bird with a Fly of 40’ can travel 40 miles per day.

The GM is recommended to use a measuring tool alongside a world map to track the actual location of each Party on the move, as well as to determine distances traveled.

The table of multipliers below is used for determining the effective distance traveled when moving through rough/obstructed terrain.

TypeExamplesDistance Multiplier
Clear/roadPlains, grasslands, farmland, highways & roads×1
RoughHills, desert, marsh, forest×2
HeavyDunes, forested/snowy hills, swamp×3
MountainousMountain×4
ExtremeForested/snowy mountain×5
RiverAdds +5 miles of the same terrain type it’s located in+5mi

Divide the effective distance in miles by the slowest Speed score among all traveling Party members. Remember: If any part of the route is off-road, the Speed of all wheeled vehicles is halved & if any Party member is Encumbered, their Speed is halved too.

The result is the number of days until the Deadline (at which point the Party reaches the destination).

What about super short distances/high speeds?

If your Deadline before rounding is less than 1 day, you probably should have realized that instead of calculating this out — but in any case, feel free to use the value in hours if they’re for some reason critical for the game.

Visibility

Aia is a flat disc & Glass is not an exception. Your view is not limited by the curvature of the Earth. Atmospheric scattering, weather, and terrain irregularities are the primary limiter of vision. Aia has a thick atmosphere, but it isn’t that thick.

The basic determinant of view distance is observer height.

Observer PositionBase VisibilityDistinguishable at this distance
Flat ground5 milesIndividuals, buildings
Watchtower10 milesFormations, buildings, ship types
Hills20 milesArmies, towns, fleets, castles
Mountains40 milesArmies, cities, major terrain
Peak80 milesOnly the largest features

Multiply by the following (all armies carry telescopes):

  • Hazy: ×0.5
  • Foggy: ×0.25
  • No telescope: ×0.5

Advancement

When one of the Factions (p. @) you’re the Leader of or a cult Faction dedicated to you grows in Families past the minimum Families threshold required to support a Leader of the next Level up, AND you do not yet have as many Class Features as your highest Faction Level, you can pick a Class from those you qualify for (as defined by what you have done recently & what Factions you currently lead) to gain a Class Feature from according to that Class’s method.

There are two methods of qualifying for a Class:

  • Do something heroic since the last time you gained a Level that unarguably reflects the nature of that Class.
  • The Faction that gained enough Followers to qualify you for the next Level has a government type (p. @) that explicitly qualifies you for that Class.

Taking Control of a Bigger Faction

If you are given control of a large Faction, you don’t get any extra Class Features for it. You only gain a single Class Feature when:

  • Your largest controlled Faction’s Level increases.
  • Through guile, wit, & brute force, you seize control of a Faction larger than yours against the will of its current owner.

For this reason, apprentices to power are normally handed smaller responsibilities to govern & grow first. It’s why all aristocracy deliberately engages in frivolous & ritualized warfare. This is also one of the reasons why regencies & abrupt transitions of power are unstable periods.

Loss of Levels

Your Factions can be destroyed. If this happens, you don’t personally lose your Class Features — but you won’t get any more until you build a new Faction up to meet & exceed the Level of your previous one.

You DO, however, lose the benefits of boosted HP while healing, since your greatest Level is now much smaller. If you lose your Faction but not your current HP, guard it zealously — you won’t be able to raise it that high again for quite a while.

Resources

A Resource is a strategically valuable material produced only at certain Locations (p. @), which are called Resource Nodes.

Obtaining Resources

Glass Empires deliberately eschews complex modeling of commodities exchange markets on the basis that emulating a proper dynamic economy is nearly impossible for computers, let alone a GM’s brain. The point of the game is not individual acts of arbitrage!

As a result, we use a simplified model. We assume the following things:

  1. The GM determines & places Resource Nodes at the time of building the campaign world.
  2. Resource Nodes, when made into Settlements, produce 1 or more copies of a Resource.
  3. Access to a Resource is a binary yes/no. We do not measure or combine sums. Each copy is a sufficient supply for a single sovereign Faction.
  4. If a Faction has one of the following, they have access to the Resource:
    • Direct control over a Resource Node producing that specific Resource.
    • An agreement with a Faction who has a copy of that Resource to have that copy continuously shipped via a currently unblocked trade route.
    • Another Leader in the same sovereign realm who has met one of these other conditions.
  5. A Faction with no access suffers penalties based on the Resource in question.

Design Implications

GMs should note that this design implicitly means that a single copy is always sufficient for an empire of any size. There are various compromises you can try to make here (i.e. Resource Nodes that are only good up to a certain Level of Faction), but most will be quite ugly. For the sake of the core game, try ~1 Resource copies per 10,000 square miles on your campaign map.

Types of Resource

There are two types of Resource:

  • Ordinary: If you have no access, all Deadlines & items that would logically require that resource have their cost/necessary Deadline duration doubled.
  • Exotic: As Ordinary, but costs increase 10-fold.

Ordinary Resources are assumed to be abundant in the world, such that alternative trade routes & distant shipping could be arranged even if it came at great friction. Exotic Resources are assumed to be produced only by a handful of locations & in great demand.

Trade Routes

A trade route is a path along connected, unblocked roads or naval routes between a Resource & a target Location.

  • Connected means a continuous unbroken line of roads. Trade demands roads, even if it’s a simple dirt trail. Navigable rivers & open waters are included.
  • Unblocked means no hostile army occupies the road or is within a 50-mile radius of a traced route across the seas.

Resources on Glass

In Glass, there are two Resources in play, both of which are Ordinary:

  • Wood: High-quality timber required for ships & siege machines.
    • Impacts shipbuilding, siege duration, & lumber construction.
  • Metal: Needed for almost everything. All of Glass’s mines are managed by salamander slave clans.
    • Impacts Army raising time & all construction/manufacturing/fortifications.

Game Design Notes

Why Wood & Metal?

We track Wood because Aian warfare is heavily dependent on siege machinery due to extensive fortifications, & Metal because salamanders hold a total monopoly on mining in Glass — as a consequence, we must track the leverage they hold, but need not differentiate in type of metal as not only are all critical, but all are commanded by the same group.

Distributed Resources

We might refer to the standard Resource described here as a monopoly Resource, as specific Locations are assumed to have a monopoly on their production. However, some cases will require tracking of the availability of generalized materials such as food — which, notably, is produced by every Settlement in some quantity & is desired by those who cannot grow it.

Rather than tracking these explicitly, we generally use Features for Faction Types that demand access to one or more friendly Settlements exceeding a certain size. The Families metric of a Settlement is, in a real sense, the resource volume for such distributed/generalized resources.

What About Magical Resources?

Here’s a fun note for adventurous GMs with homebrewed Resources: Theoretically, control over a given Resource could grant any benefit desired to Factions so long as it reflected the underlying fiction. Magical rocks that are used in cool guns? Only those Factions can mix in cool gun units. Fancy construction materials? Construction bonus instead of a penalty. Cheaper castles & housing. All you have to do is plot out all of the logical consequences & use-cases of a given material’s existence & estimate how they should show up in the rules.
