## @ Locations

You should create a 1d6 random table of at least 6 distinct Locations for each distinct area, biome, or region in play. This table is used for small, hidden/secretive, or outwardly unremarkable landmarks or objects that are flexible in placement.

<Callout type="gm" heading="Tip">
By not marking a location-independent Location ahead of time, you can minimize prep work required to start playing & simply regenerate the list as it gets used up.
</Callout>

Locations can be a variety of things, but are generally *small*, *hidden/secretive*, or *outwardly unremarkable* landmarks or objects. Examples may include but aren't limited to small castles/forts, the homes of colorful NPCs, graveyards or battle sites, merchants or artisans of particular character, dungeons, secret treasures, ruins, potential settlement sites, or locations of intense immaterial value/significance to one or more existing worldly Factions.

You only need a new separate table for each area that has a *wildly distinct character* in terms of what can be found there (i.e. deserts vs. tundras vs. jungles). Major differences in cultures (i.e. cultures whose homes are built underground) may also justify the creation of a separate table.

Whenever the game requires that a *new & unmarked* Location be brought into play (i.e. as a consequence of the Travel Move), roll on your Locations table for that area to figure out what Location in question is the topic. From that point onward, the Location is marked as being at the spot where it was discovered. Remove it from your table & replace the entry with a new Location.

## Generator: Random Locations

The GM may use this Location generator if he has no Random Locations table suitable for a given region, area, or biome, or to generate new entries to fill out a region-specific table.

1. Roll d66 on the Location table & then on the Location Status table, then determine Faction ownership.
2. If the location/status result is nonsense for the area & you can't figure out how to marry it to the fiction, discard the Location. Nothing is found.

Random Locations may not be immediately actionable, but they always stick around. Mark them on the map after they're discovered.

### Determining Faction Ownership

Locations are generally owned. The GM decides one of the following *on the spot* based on narrative logic:

- **If only a single major Faction would plausibly claim this**: It's a Lv1d6 Subfaction of theirs.
- **If multiple Factions would or did fight over it**: Assign odds to a d6 & roll to determine owner, contestants, etc.
- **If it's too remote, worthless, hidden, or obscure**: It's a new independent Lv1d6 Faction.
- **If unsure**: It's abandoned.

### @ Cults

Settlements have 1d6−1 major cult Factions, minimum 1.

Roll 1d6 per cult to determine their shares of the Settlement's Families, out of 80%. The remaining 20% are unclaimed & donate their attention to other forces, if at all. Round share-based division however is convenient to calculate. If the resulting Families sums are below 20 Families each, merge cults together, starting from the smallest into the second-smallest & so on up.

<Callout type="example">
In a town of 1,000 Families, you determine there are 3 cults. You roll 1d6 for each & get 2, 4, and 1. Since only 80% or 800 of all the Families are available, you divide 800 seven ways (4+2+1 = 7) & get approximately 110 per share. In other words, the biggest cult has 440 Families (its Leader is Lv7), the second smallest has 220 (Lv6), and the smallest has 110 (Lv6).

In a town of 520 Families, you determine there are 5 cults, with 6, 6, 4, 1, & 1 shares each. Each share is ~20 people, so the bottom 2 cults are both merged into the next largest cult, producing 3 cults of approximately the same size — around 140 Families each. That's 420 total, leaving ~100 Families unaffiliated.
</Callout>
