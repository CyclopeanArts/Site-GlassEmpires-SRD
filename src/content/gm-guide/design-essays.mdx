A series of design essays that guided the development of Glass Empires.

## The Existence of Literal Genies

In a world where gods:

- Exist;
- Have limited time & attention;
- Can be contacted from anywhere;
- Have many worshipers making frivolous pleas;
- Require those worshipers to stay loyal for any reason;

Gods *will* be blamed for failing to show up when a petitioner is in need. This can potentially cascade & prohibit gods of exceptional power whose rituals allow perfectly unambiguous contact & information transmission methods to become increasingly at risk against smaller, more local competitors who can serve needs quickly, accurately & at length.

For example, imagine Zeus as the Father of all Gods being pulled every which way across the Mediterranean, especially if we assume he physically exists & cannot manifest on-the-spot with fast-as-light movement. If he communicated by literally manifesting before you & saying "No," he would be at a disadvantage because it would be very obvious whenever he *didn't* show up. Humans are *far more spiteful* over a clear failure or refusal than for a miscommunication or ambiguity.

The peasant cannot see the volume of requests & worldly complexity that Zeus faces. What he thinks instead is: "*I cannot reach Zeus. I sacrificed that goat on his altar for NOTHING. I have been ROBBED.*" He becomes a very angry customer.

As a result, **gods may *deliberately* introduce contact rituals that are opaque & noisy so that interpretation becomes a difficult art**. This minimizes points of contact & potentially creates false signals which works in favor of confirmation bias. A 50% false signal feels like only a 25% error rate, assuming that a false signal is correct 50% of the time — & humans are *exceptionally* bad at handling or understanding probabilistic outcomes.

## A Simplified Economic Trade Model

Rather than emulate the inputs & outputs of every Location in the game & their necessary demands & productive centers, we can observe an essential tenant key to any population: **Everyone must be economically sustainable**.

There must be inputs & outputs. Furthermore, traders will optimize for functionality.

If we assume that the system is stable & functional — which is a *big* caveat, especially during times of war & unrest — then we can simplify trade into something resembling the following approximate structure:

- You buy Trade Goods at a Settlement. You have a target Settlement in mind.
- If sold at that place, it produces a random but usually profitable profit margin based on distance.
- If sold elsewhere, it produces a random profit margin that is weighted lower, such that it is usually not profitable accounting for risk.

Consider that this system has *at least* the following flaws:

- You can't capture anomalies in production level.
- You can't capture disruptions in other risk factors that don't directly relate to distance. What if the only route is exceptionally dangerous? What if it's safe & stable?
- You can't capture obvious similarities in inputs/outputs. What if a village has identical needs to the original destination?

Simplifications fail to capture edge cases as per usual. (We might note that arguably, the areas where a simplification *fails* are *more interesting* than anything else.) In any case, I believed that this framework is only suitable for a game where detailed economic & trade warfare is *not* expected to play a prominent role — in fact, that it may be best left out altogether, then, to avoid distracting players with the promise of something that isn't meant to be a meaningful component. Why over-complicate matters?

## Glass Empires' Faction System Only Works for Pre-industrial Societies

The only reason the implicit economic simulation in Glass Empires functions is because we have made massive simplifications based on an implicit model of human society. Most critically, **we assume that human population has a tight relationship to economic productivity**. This is the key assumption that the Industrial Age *rips to shreds*.

**In addition, we assume that population centers correspond tightly to power**, which is why we can get away with assuming a Capital's population is reflective of the rest of its power. This is true for almost all of history but decouples more extensively the more industrialized a state becomes.

**Our assumptions are also dependent on a relatively inefficient tribal power structure**. And in addition to real-world tribal & factional factors, Aia has several compounding confounding factors that prohibit centralization of power:

- Absence of true firearms keeps cheap stone fortifications viable
- Highly decentralized mail & courier service courtesy of the local bird-brains prohibits rulers from monopolizing high-speed communications;
  Hostile & dangerous wilderness teeming with inexplicable & deadly magic provides continual "shatterzone" pressures from nearly every wild region;
- Realms artificially sustained through a single ruler's combat & magical prowess often struggles to survive a succession crisis;
- And more still!

In the context of Aia, none of this is a problem. The Grand Disc is not yet near an industrial revolution ... although if you ran out the clock another couple hundred years, it might get there. *But* if you set about deploying the Glass Empires ruleset in another setting, be careful of the assumptions you import.

It is worth keeping in mind as a GM that the Lunarians (p. @) do not operate on a clear-cut economy at all & that attempting to model their existence using the Faction system is a terribly flawed route to pursue. They are rather like an odd endgame bonus stage, in this sense.
